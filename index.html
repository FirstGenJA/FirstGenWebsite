Utah Japantown Collective

Amplifying the collective Japanese American voice of SLC through intergenerational collaboration that promotes the diverse voices of Japantown’s past, present, and future.

Vision

Formed in April 2024, SLC NextGen JA emerged after Senate Bill 272 passed and news broke about the NHL and Sports Entertainment, Culture, and Convention District. As a group of friends who grew up attending temple and church events on Japantown Street, we got together to discuss how we could help advocate and organize our community efforts to preserve and revitalize Japantown. We gathered community leaders to address how these changes impact Japantown and to ensure our voices are heard in the revitalization of Downtown.

History of Nihonmachi: Salt Lake City Japantown

The Early Years

1890s-1960s

Japantown started to grow around 1910 after the first Japanese arrived in the late 1800s to work on the railroads and farms. It would continue to grow before World War II, and despite the setbacks to the community during the war, Japantown recovered and remained the heart of the Japanese American community into the 1960s.

The Dispersion

1960s

Through eminent domain and the seizing of 90 Japanese Americans-owned or operated businesses, the move to create a new convention space, the Salt Palace, decimated Japantown. Only the Salt Lake Buddhist Temple and Japanese Church of Christ remained on 100 South.

Preservation Efforts

1990s-2010s

The Japanese Community Preservation Committee (JCPC) was formed to advocate for creating and preserving a garden as a buffer between the Salt Palace and the Japanese Church of Christ. As a part of this effort, 100 S. was officially named Japantown Street, and plans were made to redesign the streetscape to reflect Japantown.

Present Day

2020s

Senate Bill (SB) 272 established a Capital City Revitalization Zone extending from the Delta Center to Japantown (100 S and between 200 W and 300 W) and introduces a 0.5% citywide sales tax increase, generating $900 million to fund the Smith Entertainment Group’s (SEG) Sports Entertainment Convention and Culture District (SECC). This project will affect Japantown Street and daily operations at the Japanese Church of Christ (JCC) and Salt Lake Buddhist Temple (SLBT).

JCC Memorial

The JCC Memorial, held on September 21st, was a poignant gathering dedicated to honoring the legacy of the Jewish Community Center (JCC) and its historical significance in the community. The event brought together individuals and families to reflect on the contributions the JCC made to cultural and social life in the area. During the memorial, attendees shared personal stories and memories, highlighting the JCC's role as a hub for community engagement, education, and celebration. Activities included speeches from community leaders, a memorial service, and opportunities for participants to engage in discussions about the importance of preserving such spaces for future generations.
